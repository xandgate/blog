I have a note from three years ago that says "look into this more." That's the whole note. No context. No link. Just a command to a version of me who apparently knew what "this" was.

I've been running some version of a personal knowledge system for about six years. It started as a folder of text files. Then Notion. Then Obsidian. Now Logseq, which I actually use every day in a way I didn't use the others. The core premise has always been the same: capture everything, connect it later, never lose a thought.

The problem is I'm not sure it's working the way I thought it would.

## The Anxiety of the Empty Inbox

There's a particular kind of anxiety that note-taking systems create in me that I didn't have before I started using them. It's the feeling that something is happening — a conversation, a podcast, a walk where an idea showed up — and I'm not capturing it. The feeling that I'm losing something.

I didn't used to feel that way. Ideas came and went. Some stuck. Most didn't. The ones that stuck were probably the ones worth keeping.

Now I feel a mild panic at the thought of having a thought without writing it down. That doesn't seem like an improvement.

## What Notes Are Actually For

I think I confused two different things when I built my note-taking system. I thought I was building a memory. What I actually built is closer to an archive.

A memory is something you can access without effort. It surfaces when it's relevant. It connects to other things you know without you having to make the connection explicitly. Human memory is lossy and reconstructive and honestly quite good at keeping the things that matter while dropping the things that don't.

An archive is different. It keeps everything. It doesn't prioritize. It doesn't surface things on its own. It waits to be queried. And if you're not querying it — if you're not regularly reviewing, maintaining, and working the system — it becomes a very organized pile of things you wrote down and never looked at again.

Most of my notes are that. A well-tagged pile.

## The Stuff That Sticks Anyway

Here's what I've noticed: the ideas I actually use, the ones that show up in my work and in conversations and in how I think about problems — I didn't learn them from reviewing my notes. I learned them by sitting with them long enough to actually understand them. By arguing with them. By writing about them badly before I could write about them well.

The notes were part of that process, but not in the way I thought. It wasn't the act of storing the idea that made it mine. It was the friction of working through it.

The forgetting was doing something too. It was selecting. Every time an idea didn't survive the night, it was probably because it wasn't as interesting as it felt in the moment. Every time something came back — kept recurring in different forms across weeks or months — that was the system working. Not the software. The one in my head.

## What I'm Trying Now

I'm not quitting Logseq. I still find genuine value in having a place to think out loud in text, to connect things across time, to not lose the actual artifact of work in progress. But I'm trying to change what I think the system is for.

The new rule: I capture things not to preserve them but to process them. A note isn't a backup. It's a draft. If I haven't done anything with a note in six months, it probably deserved to be forgotten and I should let it go.

The anxiety about losing things is still there. I'm trying to sit with it instead of immediately resolving it by opening a new note.

There's probably something in that discomfort worth keeping. I just haven't written it down yet.
