There's a specific feeling I've had since I was a junior developer that I'm having less often now. It's the feeling of knowing something. Not looking it up. Not generating it. Actually knowing it — the kind of knowing where the answer is already there before the question is fully formed.

I used to get that feeling about CSS specificity rules, about how Drupal's hook system worked, about which PHP functions had off-by-one quirks you had to memorize if you didn't want bugs at the edges. Small things. But they accumulated into something that felt like fluency. You stop translating and start thinking in the language.

I'm not sure I'm building that anymore. I'm not sure I need to.

## The Thing About Fluency

When I was learning to drive, I had to think about every gear change. It was effortful and conscious and I was aware of the gap between what I was doing and what experienced drivers did without thinking. Then at some point the gap closed and I stopped being aware of the gear changes at all.

That's what expertise felt like to me. A progressive automation of the things you used to have to consciously hold. The working memory freed up for the harder parts.

But there's something else about fluency that I didn't appreciate until recently: it's how you notice when something is wrong. When you're fluent, anomalies surface. The gear that doesn't engage quite right. The line of code that technically works but feels off. The architecture that's structurally sound but smells like it's going to be expensive in two years.

That noticing is pattern recognition built on a lot of internalized repetition. I'm not sure it transfers when you outsource the repetition.

## What AI Is Actually Replacing

I've been thinking about this wrong. The framing I kept reaching for was "AI is replacing knowledge workers" or "AI is replacing coding" — the big, career-level threat narrative. But that's not what I actually experience day to day.

What I experience is more specific: AI is replacing the parts of my work that felt like expertise but were actually just retrieval. Looking up the correct syntax. Remembering which configuration option does which thing. Holding a large codebase in working memory long enough to find the right place to make a change.

That stuff felt like expertise because it was hard and because not everyone could do it quickly. But it wasn't judgment. It was a very sophisticated form of pattern-matched retrieval. And retrieval is exactly what language models are good at.

The judgment parts — whether this is the right architecture, whether this tradeoff is worth making, whether the person asking for this feature actually wants what they're asking for — those are still mine. They're harder to articulate, which maybe means they're harder to replace. Or maybe it just means we haven't figured out how to ask for them yet.

## The Uncomfortable Part

Here's what I actually sit with: I don't know if I'm maintaining the substrate that makes the judgment possible.

The judgment I have is built on years of retrieval. Of looking things up the hard way and making the connection myself. Of being wrong and having to understand why. Of building mental models through repetition and friction.

If I skip all of that — if every question gets answered before I've had time to be confused by it — am I still building the thing that makes the judgment good? Or am I spending down a capital I'm no longer accumulating?

I genuinely don't know. I'm aware that this could be a version of "things were better when they were harder," which is a bad argument. The car didn't make people worse at navigating; it just changed what navigation meant. Maybe this is the same.

But I also notice that the developers I most want to work with are the ones who have clearly been confused by hard things for a long time. Who have the scars. Who know where the bodies are buried not because they looked it up but because they buried some of them.

I don't know how you build that without the retrieval. I don't know if you still need to.

I'm writing this partly to find out if I have a view on it. I'm not sure I do yet.
