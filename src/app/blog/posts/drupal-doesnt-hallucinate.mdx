Sometime in 2024, machines quietly became the majority of your audience.

Not scrapers gaming SEO. AI systems reasoning over your content in real time — on behalf of people who may never load your homepage — to answer questions you will never see asked.

That is not a traffic problem. It is a trust problem. When an AI tells someone what your organisation does, misstates eligibility criteria, or cites a policy you retired eighteen months ago, the failure does not attach to the model. It attaches to you.

**When a machine reasons over what you have published, what does it find?**

Last week I was at the FOST Drupal AI Summit in New York — developers, architects, product leads, all circling the same anxiety. The demos were competent. The case studies were sharper. What stayed with me was not a feature announcement. It was a reframing: the organisations that spent decades building structured content did not get lucky. They got ready.

![One of the opening slides at the FOST Drupal AI Summit put the choice plainly.](/images/blog/fost-same-old-crazy-new.jpg)

## The audience you did not plan for

Here is the finding that stopped several summit conversations cold: publishers who blocked AI crawlers lost roughly [**7% of weekly human traffic**](https://ppc.land/blocking-ai-crawlers-cost-news-publishers-7-of-traffic-study-finds/) within six weeks, according to a Wharton/Rutgers study. Blocking the machines cost them real readers. Machine visibility and human visibility are no longer separable bets.

The scale behind that number: in 2024, automated traffic [surpassed human traffic for the first time in a decade](https://www.imperva.com/resources/resource-library/reports/2025-bad-bot-report/) — 51% of global web traffic, per Imperva's Bad Bot Report. By 2025 it had [reached 53%](https://securitybrief.co.uk/story/bots-make-up-53-of-web-traffic-thales-report-says), with automated traffic growing [eight times faster](https://www.humansecurity.com/newsroom/2026-state-of-ai-traffic-cyberthreat-benchmark-report/) than human traffic, according to HUMAN Security.

The AI-specific split matters more than the headline. [Cloudflare's 2025 crawler analysis](https://blog.cloudflare.com/from-googlebot-to-gptbot-whos-crawling-your-site-in-2025/) recorded GPTBot — OpenAI's training crawler — up 305% year-on-year. ChatGPT-User rose 2,825%. GPTBot crawls to *train* models. ChatGPT-User visits *while reasoning* — mid-conversation, to answer a specific question. Your content is being consulted now, by systems you did not invite, to produce answers you will not review.

The human traffic picture is already shifting. [Pew Research found](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/03/18/ai-overviews-in-search/) that when an AI summary appeared in search, users clicked through to a source only 8% of the time, versus 15% without one. [Gartner and iO Digital](https://press.iodigital.com/io-predicts-organic-search-traffic-to-plummet-50-by-2028-as-ai-transforms-customer-behaviour) project a 50% decline in organic search traffic by 2028.

Your content strategy was built for human readers. That audience is now a minority — and shrinking.

## Hallucination is a retrieval problem wearing a brand name

The dramatic failures get the headlines: invented citations, fabricated court cases. The expensive failures are quieter. A plausible answer that is *almost* right — drawn from content that is outdated, thin on context, or structurally ambiguous.

The fix is rarely a better model. It is better source material.

Most production assistants work through retrieval: search a corpus, synthesise an answer from what comes back. Answer quality tracks retrieval quality. Content with typed fields, consistent vocabulary, and explicit relationships between entities retrieves cleanly. Clean retrieval produces accurate answers. The architecture decisions your organisation made five years ago are now shaping the AI responses that cite you — whether you intended that or not.

Organisations that built on Drupal — enforced content types, taxonomies, editorial workflows — inherited that foundation. Many others are still improvising with rich text and hope.

## The room went quiet: EU Careers

At the summit, EPSO — the European Personnel Selection Office — made the abstract concrete.

They run EU Careers, the hiring portal for the European Commission. Their assistant, Epsy, is grounded in content managed in Drupal: competition procedures, eligibility criteria, account types, selection stages — maintained by editorial teams with clear ownership, not scraped from the open web and prayed over.

Try it at [eu-careers.europa.eu/en/testing-ai](https://eu-careers.europa.eu/en/testing-ai). The first question is not small talk. It asks which competition type, which stage, which account. That is not friction. That is a system that knows its content has layers and refuses to flatten them.

It also states plainly that answers may not always be fully accurate, and asks for feedback to improve. Not boilerplate. A design choice: helpful, fallible, accountable to the editorial process behind it.

The CMS did not merely store content. It enforced the standards that made deployment defensible.

## When the CMS becomes an endpoint

The Drupal AI Initiative's dual strategy maps onto this shift — and it is a strategy, not a feature slide.

**Inside-out:** AI embedded in the authoring experience. Field-level translation, summarisation, rewriting. ECA workflows for tagging and metadata. A toolbar assistant that keeps editors in context. The goal is velocity without surrendering approval — humans still ship what ships.

**Outside-in:** implementation of the Model Context Protocol (MCP). A Drupal site with MCP enabled becomes a queryable endpoint. An external agent retrieves structured data in real time, takes actions within the permission model you already defined. Access controls, content types, and taxonomies carry over. The AI does not see what the user should not see.

That reframes the hallucination debate. Instead of training once and hoping knowledge stays current, you give reasoning systems live, permission-aware access to a maintained store. The answer is as fresh as your last editorial update. This is not a chatbot bolted onto a homepage. It is infrastructure designed to be reasoned over.

## The advantage that does not deprecate on a quarterly cycle

There is a harder argument worth making plainly.

When a change is proposed to Drupal core, it goes through review — multiple contributors, community consensus, documented rationale. Slow by every metric Silicon Valley uses to judge software velocity. Also the reason organisations with serious compliance requirements — government, healthcare, regulated finance — have chosen Drupal for twenty years.

The contrast with AI-generated code is not that AI skips review. You can wrap any process around generated output. The contrast is what the review *carries*. A 2018 core patch includes the reasoning behind a permission-model change. Edge cases live in the issue queue. Security tradeoffs in the thread. That institutional memory does not transfer to an automated linter. When you inherit a Drupal codebase, you inherit the thinking that produced it.

That kind of trust takes decades to build. It cannot be purchased as velocity.

![A Pantheon presenter frames where AI actually sits on the hype curve right now — past peak expectations, not yet at the plateau of productivity.](/images/blog/fost-hype-cycle.jpg)

**The honest counterargument:** Drupal is not the only CMS with structured content. Contentful, Sanity, and others offer typed models and API-first delivery. The Drupal-specific case rests on community review, depth of government adoption, and mature permission and workflow systems — not typed fields alone. If your organisation does not need those constraints, the argument narrows.

## What to do before your next rebuild conversation

The question from clients has shifted. It used to be: *should we rebuild on something more modern?* It is increasingly: *how do we make our content legible to machines without breaking trust with humans?*

The answer is the boring one architects have argued for years: typed fields, clean taxonomies, content separated from presentation, workflows with named owners. Clients wanted to move fast and structure later.

Later arrived.

**Three moves worth making now:**

1. **Audit your content types.** Are fields typed? Are taxonomies consistent? Are relationships explicit? If meaning lives in freeform prose and visual layout, machines cannot reliably retrieve it.

2. **Review robots.txt with intent.** Blanket blocking of AI crawlers has a documented cost to human traffic. Distinguish training crawlers from retrieval crawlers. Know what you are blocking and why.

3. **Ask your CMS vendor about MCP.** A vague answer tells you how seriously they are treating machine-mediated access to your content.

---

Here is the irony the Drupal community should own without apology: Drupal was never designed for machine consumption. It was built for editorial teams managing complex content under regulatory pressure — teams that needed structure because *people* required accountability. Those constraints felt heavy in the age of move-fast iteration. They are exactly what machines reward now.

The web changed around Drupal. Drupal did not need to catch up. It was already there.
